An introduction

To my friends I am Tony Pink, officially born and christened Anthony Edgar Pink. I was born at 58 Rowden Road, Beckenham, Kent, just opposite Clock House Station and beside Cater Park, on a Tuesday Morning, 11th September 1934 at 5.30 am ... Mum said I came with the Milkman. My dad told me about himself at odd times. I was always embarrassed by him singing 'Bless this House' or other pieces. He really enjoyed classical music, opera, football, cricket and other manly pursuits in all of which I seemed dreadfully lacking. His father, also Edgar, was a baker journeyman and for a hobby played euphonium in Croydon Silver Band. He died when my dad was only eleven. My dad lived at 78 Laurel Grove, Penge, SE20 . He went to work after only a very basic education. He told me he used to clean up and sweep hair from the floor of the gents hairdressers at Crystal Palace to get enough money to go skating.

He had served in the first world war as a runner in the 10th Royal West Surrey Regt.. and was wounded somewhere in France. We have photographs showing him with other chaps 'convalescing on the Norfolk Broads. (Whilst looking at his medals I noticed Sid Makriel (my father in law) also had two medals which gave Sid's number and Regiment.... Pte Sidney Makriel 45743 Rifle Brigade.)

After the first world war Edgar James became the publisher of the London Mercury. (Archie Veryard had all the copies my dad had. wrapped by the publisher in brown paper) This was a private publication to publicise the works of budding poets and unknown literary genius'. He worked directly for the owner, Sir John Squires, and later was employed at the Field and Queen at 229 the Strand, which is why we have two water colours by T.S. Boys depicting the Strand from opposite directions, and which show his office.

Before I was born he worked for a while in the depression of 1926 driving a coach with tourists through the muck and bullets area of France (Frames Tours). Photo's supports this fact. He was then was out of work for two years before being employed by, first the Evening Standard and then, as I remember the Sunday Pictorial. (price 2d.)

Auntie Pat Pratt, (Bert Canty's secretary) bought me an airforce blue suit with two pairs of trousers in which I was photographed wearing a paper hat and holding a wooden sword, and some other photographs were taken at the “Mirror” in a sailor suit sitting on my dad's lap or standing solo. He used to go to the races in Ostend with one Charlie Little (Liverpool & Vic Insurance Man I believe) and when the second war broke out we were in Middlekirk, Belgium and only got home on the last boat to England from Zeebrugge.

Dad went on to be the Chief London Sales Representative and worked in Central London throughout the war, often not coming home until Monday evening having gone to work on a Friday, although he went to work other days too, his main job kept him in London through the weekend. One such night he came to the end of Pickhurst Rise, where we lived, and was told that the street had been flattened and that he could not go in. He came home by various alley ways and back gardens and found us all OK. I remember going to school and walking round huge craters in the road with gas mains burning and people frying their breakfasts in frying pans over the burning flames down the crater because the gas indoors was not working. Also there was a pillar box up against a front door and letters all over the garden. Five years after the second war ended he found his job confining, and applied to be a wholesale newspaper distributor and secured the agency for Strood, succeeding the retiring Mrs Pierson at 93 High Street Strood in 1950. He soon won back some of the lost customers from Surridge Dawson. Moved from 93 High street to 49 Cuxton Road and then planned the new depot at Station Approach but died following an operation to remove a cancer in his colon in 1960 not seeing the rewards of his labours.

I attended HAWSE DOWN Junior School with Miss Robinson being the Head teacher. No sooner had I started there war broke out, and I can vividly remember whilst my dad was at work, Mr Hayward from 113 coming over when the sirens sounded for a practice, and made us all get under the dining room table. We never hid under there again but dad moved the player piano adjacent to the main wall of the house and we slept on a mattress beside the piano if the bombing got heavy. Failing that, we stayed in our own beds. One night we were downstairs and a 500 lb bomb landed in old McDonald's back garden (No 107). It used to be a picture of DIG for VICTORY !! The following morning we had a bath full of carrots, cabbage etc., plus John's pillow had a bomb splinter through it right where his head would have been if he had slept there that night. That must have been the luckiest night of his life. A bomb splinter lodged itself in the spine of one of the copies of London Mercury, having passed clean through the wooden cupboard.

Early on in the war we went to live with friends of Dad at Sandy Nr. Bedford, just for a week or two I believe. I remember shitting myself on the way to school as I was wearing a 'siren suit' and my dad had tried out on us for the first time ' SURE SHIELD LAXATIVES' - I can promise you they work !! After the Doodle-bugs which we all weathered, the V2 's caused our School to Evacuate to Skellow, Nr DONCASTER and I was there at 60 Crossfield Lane with Bill and Betty YAPP and their son RAYMOND. Most of the kids came home within a week, but I stayed there about 10 months and came home after the VE Party to enjoy another one at the TOC 'H' Hall in West Wickham. I was the only one with a Yorkshire accent, and my dad now wore horn rimmed specs!! He drove a FORD PREFECT (Maroon) Reg No. FLY 409.

I won a scholarship to the Beckenham & Penge County School for Boys and had to make a new set of friends yet again. I used to travel to school on the 8.16 from West Wickham to Clock House, and meet my uncle 'Buller' (George William Bradley) at the next stop, Eden Park. He worked for London Transport. I must find out as what? He was very smartly dressed, rolled umbrella and all that! I was quite proud to be accepted there. On reflection I had a wonderful five years of Sea Cadet training, cricket and football after school and generally doing what boys do when growing up. I started in form 1d, and after that we were 'streamed' I went into 2G which stood for 'German' I stuck it for about a year, but Herr Doktor Liepman had zilch control over us and the class was often a shambles. - more of that later. I later transferred from 'G' to 'H' which was for handicrafts. I did both woodwork and metalwork, but by far favoured the woodwork. Mr Waterman was the kindly teacher, and only when severely stretched would he hurl the wooden black board cleaner at the offending child! I still find it useful to recall the correct method to use a marking gauge, or a chisel to cut a joint, not mention a spoke-shave!

When we moved to Gillingham on May 1st 1950, I still had one term to conclude my schooling, and sit the School Certificate Examination. When I left school I thought I was going to have my summer holidays as usual, but Dad said Oh no! and I started work the following Monday. Six days a week for 25/=d. I had to give Mum a pound for my keep, which left just 5/=d for myself. Dad didn’t realise I smoked. He would have been livid if he had known! 10 Weight’s cost 10½d. So you could see my back was to the wall from the word go! However, the first day at work ended with me being taken off to hospital, and my appendix being removed at 7.30 the next morning.
