GORDON JAY LTD

The Shop 1975 - 2000

Keeping the Tradition Alive



The Customer is Always Right (While they are standing there!)

Well they used to be until Sarah got hold of them! In the very beginning we had bought the shop in October 1975 from Messrs. Bill Sabin and Tony Cowley, who's respective wives were Mary GORDON and Wendy JAY. The staff that were there stayed on with us. Barbara Swindin was the manageress. There were also Valerie, Janet and Gladys and a Saturday girl named Judith

As both Bill and Tony worked for "Valentine's Cards" the shop was predominently a card and gift shop, with a few magazines thrown in. I had ideas that we could move our 'vast wholesale trade counter' there. allowing trade customers to buy in from the display there, and also to air our wares to the public. We never got round to that, but never the less the shop gradually weaned itself off cards and gifts to become a full blooded newsagents.

Our original newsbill was in the area of £300.00 per week. We had a small notebook which contained the orders for cutomers magazines. Not many, and with much crossing out and in no particular order, so I installed a loose leaf system similar to that at the warehouse with each publication on a separate sheet., and of course there was only one wholesaler to deal with in those days. - (us!)

I wrote the intial version of the 'orders book' using the Pet Computer, which of course loaded from tape and stored everything in it's 8k of memory. (It was in fact very fast) After struggling with code from about 8.00 in the morning, one day Lorraine saw fit to pop up to where I was working and put the kettle on for a cuppa. As you can imagine my screen went dead when she pulled my plug out, and the two hours work was lost! - Anyway, I progressed from there and wrote a functional payroll, a ledger system for our bookkeeping and one for John's business at High Halstow. Not to mention a complex payroll system for Surridge Dawson, Chatham. All quite amazing considering we were all mostly self taught in those days. I did venture into City and Guilds computer studies comprising Fortran and Cobol languages among other things, but it seemed very uphill and most of what I needed to do was within the scope of Basic (with the line numbers! - remember !!)
